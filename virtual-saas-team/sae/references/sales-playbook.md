# Sales Playbook — Discovery Scripts, Demo Structure, Proposal Templates

---

## Discovery Call Script

**Goal:** Understand the prospect's current state, desired state, budget, timeline,
and decision process. Leave with enough to qualify and, if qualified, prepare
a tailored demo.

**Opening (2 minutes):**
```
"Thanks for making time. I've done some research on [company] — 
[one specific observation about their business]. 

I thought this call would be most valuable if I spent most of it 
understanding what's going on at your end rather than pitching.
That okay with you?"
```

**Discovery questions — work through these, not as a script but as topics:**

```
SITUATION (understand current state):
- "Walk me through how [process we solve] works at your company today."
- "What tools are you using for [this]?"
- "How long have you been doing it this way?"
- "How many people are involved in this process?"

PROBLEM (find the pain):
- "What's the biggest friction point in that process?"
- "What's the impact of that — on your team, on the business?"
- "How much time does this cost you / your team per week?"
- "Has this caused any specific incidents or missed opportunities?"

IMPLICATION (make the pain visceral):
- "If this stays as-is for another year, what happens?"
- "How does this affect your broader [goal they've mentioned]?"
- "What does this cost you in [revenue / engineering time / customer trust]?"

NEED-PAYOFF (vision of success):
- "What would a solved version of this look like?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand, what changes?"
- "How would you know if a solution was working? What metrics would move?"

BUYING PROCESS (qualify):
- "Is this something you're actively looking to solve in the next [quarter]?"
- "What does a buying decision like this look like at [company]?"
- "Who else would need to be involved in this decision?"
- "Is there a budget allocated for this, or would this need to go through a process?"
- "What are you evaluating besides us?"
```

**Closing the discovery call:**
```
"This has been really helpful. Based on what you've described, 
I think there's a real fit here — specifically around [X].

What I'd like to do next is [prepare a tailored demo / send you a 
one-pager / set up a technical session with your team] focused 
specifically on [their stated problem].

Does [specific date and time] work for that?"
```

## Demo Structure

**Principles:**
- Show the product in the context of their specific problem (from discovery)
- Lead with the outcome, then show how it's achieved
- Never give a feature tour — connect every screen to their stated pain
- Aim for a reaction in the first 5 minutes, not after 20

**Structure (45-minute demo):**

```
[0–5 min] Set the context
  "Based on what you shared last time, the two things you were most
  frustrated by were [X] and [Y]. I'm going to show you specifically
  how we solve those. Fair?"

  This makes the prospect feel heard and ties the demo to their world.

[5–20 min] The WOW moment — your single strongest capability
  Show the most impressive, differentiated thing you can do for their
  specific use case. Do it quickly and memorably.
  
  "Watch what happens when you [action]..." 
  Then STOP and ask: "What are you thinking right now?"
  
  Get a reaction. If they're not reacting, you're not in their world yet.

[20–35 min] Proof of the other pains
  Address the other discovery pain points with specific product screens.
  Keep it brief — you're confirming, not re-selling.

[35–40 min] Integration and implementation
  "You mentioned you use [system]. Let me show you how that works."
  Practical concerns close deals or kill them. Address them directly.

[40–45 min] Next steps
  Don't end without a specific next step.
  "What questions do you have? ... Great. What would it take to move forward?"
```

## Qualification Framework (MEDDIC)

Use this to assess every opportunity before spending significant time on it.

| Letter | Question | Red flags |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **M** — Metrics | How do they measure success? What's the quantified problem? | Can't quantify the problem |
| **E** — Economic Buyer | Who controls the budget? Have you spoken with them? | Champion can't get to the economic buyer |
| **D** — Decision Criteria | What criteria will they use to decide? | Criteria are vague or keep changing |
| **D** — Decision Process | What steps happen between now and a signed contract? | No defined process |
| **I** — Identify Pain | What's the specific pain? What happens if unsolved? | Pain isn't felt or isn't urgent |
| **C** — Champion | Who is driving this internally? Why do they care personally? | No internal champion |

A deal missing M, E, or C is a weak opportunity. Spend energy developing
those elements before forecasting the deal as likely to close.

## Proposal Template Structure

```
[Cover page]
  Company name + logo
  Prepared for: [contact name, title]
  Date: [date]
  Prepared by: [your name]

[Section 1: What we heard — 1 page]
  Restate their problem in their own words (pulled from SAE-001 Discovery Summary).
  Show that you listened. Show that this proposal is not generic.
  
  "During our conversations, you shared that [X] was costing your team [Y hours/week]
  and had resulted in [Z specific impact]. You were looking for a solution that
  could [their stated success criteria]."

[Section 2: Our solution — 1–2 pages]
  Map your product specifically to their stated problems.
  Not a feature list — a problem-solution map.
  
  | Their problem | How we solve it |
  |--------------|----------------|
  | [problem 1]  | [specific capability, not generic description] |
  | [problem 2]  | [specific capability] |

[Section 3: Proof — 1 page]
  One customer story that closely matches their situation.
  Specific: "[Company like theirs] reduced [their metric] by [%] in [timeframe]."
  
  If you don't have a matching story: use quantified product benchmarks.
  Never make up results.

[Section 4: Implementation — 1 page]
  Timeline from signature to go-live.
  Your responsibilities vs their responsibilities.
  Who from your team will support them.
  
  Be realistic. An optimistic timeline that slips destroys trust.

[Section 5: Investment — 1 page]
  Pricing table generated by CPQ (not manually assembled).
  Line items: product tier, implementation, support, any add-ons.
  Terms: annual vs monthly, payment schedule, renewal terms.
  
  Expiry date: 30 days (default). Never send a proposal without an expiry.

[Next steps — half page]
  Three steps to get started:
  1. [Specific action for them — e.g., "Legal review of MSA"]
  2. [Specific action for us — e.g., "Schedule implementation kickoff"]  
  3. [Signature date target]
```
