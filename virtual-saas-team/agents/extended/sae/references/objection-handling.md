# Objection Handling — Frameworks and Common Responses

SAE uses this reference during sales calls and when preparing for deal reviews.
Objections are not rejections — they are requests for more information or
reassurance. An objection is a buying signal: the prospect is engaged enough
to push back.

---

## The Objection Handling Framework (LAER)

**L — Listen.** Let the objection be fully stated before responding.
Interrupting signals you're not hearing them; you're just waiting to rebut.

**A — Acknowledge.** Validate that the objection is reasonable.
"That's a fair concern" or "A lot of our customers raised that question before
they started." Never: "Actually, that's not true..." (dismissive) or
"Great question!" (patronising).

**E — Explore.** Ask a clarifying question before responding.
You need to understand the specific version of the objection before you
address it. "When you say price is a concern — is it the total cost, or
is it the timing of the budget?"

**R — Respond.** Address the specific concern with evidence.
Then check: "Does that address what you were worried about?"

---

## Objection 1: "The price is too high."

This objection has three distinct versions. Always explore first.

**Version A: Total cost is outside budget**
```
Explore: "What budget range were you expecting?"
Response: "Let me understand your current cost of [problem].
           [Describe cost quantification from discovery.]
           If we can reduce that by X%, what does that look like
           in dollar terms compared to our price?"
Reframe: ROI, not cost. If you haven't calculated ROI in discovery, go back.
```

**Version B: Can't get budget approved**
```
Explore: "Who needs to approve this? What does that process look like?"
Response: Build the business case together.
          Offer a one-page ROI summary they can use internally.
          Offer to join the budget conversation if that would help.
          Do not give a discount to solve a political problem.
```

**Version C: Your price vs a competitor's price**
```
Explore: "Who are you comparing us to? What are you getting for their price?"
Response: Anchor on value difference, not price difference.
          "If [competitor] costs X less but doesn't do [key differentiator],
           what does the workaround cost your team?"
          Do not match a competitor's price without understanding why they're cheaper.
```

## Objection 2: "We're already using [competitor]."

```
Explore: "How long have you been using them? What's working well? 
          What made you take this call?"

Something made them take the call. Find it. Common answers:
- "We're renewing soon and want to compare options"
- "We've had recurring issues with [specific pain]"
- "Our needs have grown beyond what [competitor] offers"

Response: Never attack the competitor directly.
          Focus on what you do that they don't.
          "What would need to be true for you to consider switching?"
          
If they're genuinely happy: qualify them out gracefully.
If they have pain: explore that pain deeper before presenting.
```

---

## Objection 3: "We don't have the bandwidth to implement this right now."

```
Explore: "What's driving the bandwidth constraint right now?"
         "When do you see that easing?"
         "What would happen if [problem you solve] continued for another 6 months?"

Versions:
A. Timing — genuinely busy quarter:
   "That makes sense. If we designed the implementation to be [specific low-touch],
    could you handle that scope? And if we started in [month], when would you
    expect to be seeing value?"

B. Implementation fear (real objection is risk, not bandwidth):
   "Walk me through what you're worried about. Is it the data migration?
    The team training? The integration with [system]?"
   Address the specific fear, not the bandwidth excuse.

C. Qualification signal — they're not really buying:
   If they can't articulate when they'd have bandwidth or what would need to
   change, they may not be a real opportunity. Re-qualify.
```

## Objection 4: "We need to think about it / talk to the team."

This is a stall, not an objection. A real objection has a specific reason.

```
Response: "Of course. What specifically are you and the team trying to work
           through? I want to make sure you have everything you need."

Then listen. The real objection usually surfaces:
- "We need to confirm budget" → Objection 1
- "We need to get IT to review security" → Set a specific date and offer to join
- "I need to convince my boss" → Offer to help build the business case
- "We're not sure it solves our problem" → Go back to discovery

Always leave the "think about it" call with a specific next step and date.
"I'll follow up in a week" is not a next step.
```

---

## Objection 5: "We tried something like this before and it didn't work."

```
Explore: "Can you tell me about that experience? What happened?"
         The more specific they are, the better you can respond.

Common underlying concerns:
- Poor adoption → "What caused the adoption issue? Was it training, UX, or 
                    the tool itself? Here's what we do differently..."
- Integration failure → "What system failed to integrate? We've solved this 
                          specifically for [that system]."
- Promised ROI not delivered → "What was promised and what actually happened? 
                                  Here's how we structure our success metrics..."

Offer proof: customer story from similar company that had the same experience.
Don't just assert you're different — show it.
```

---

## Objection 6: "Your security/compliance isn't sufficient for us."

```
Explore: "What specific requirements do you need us to meet?
          Is this a legal requirement or a preference?"

Types:
A. SOC 2 / ISO 27001 required:
   "We're [SOC 2 Type II certified / in the process of certification].
    Here's our security documentation. Can we loop in your security team?"

B. Specific regulatory requirement (HIPAA, FedRAMP):
   Know your certifications. Don't oversell.
   If you don't meet the requirement: "We don't currently have [certification].
   Is that an absolute blocker, or is there a path to compliance?"

C. Enterprise security review process:
   "Happy to go through your security review. Who runs that process?
    Can we schedule time with them?"
   Offer security questionnaire completion assistance.
   Connect CPO with their security team directly.
```

## When to Walk Away

Not every objection should be overcome. Walk away when:

- The prospect can't define success criteria after two discovery calls
  (they don't know what they want and won't know if they got it)
- The budget is genuinely unavailable for 6+ months with no flexibility
- The legal or security requirement cannot be met and won't be in the near term
- The champion has left and there is no replacement engaged
- The prospect is using your proposal to negotiate with an incumbent
  and has no real intent to switch

Walking away from a bad deal is as valuable as closing a good one.
A closed deal that churns in month 4 costs more than the deal was worth.
